The Internet

What even is the internet?

The internet is a series of connected devices. These include phones, laptops, servers, smartfridges, and broadly anything that can talk to any other device.

How does the internet work?

The internet works by first splitting up data into chunks, called packets, then passing these packets from router to router until they reach their destination.

Why split up the data?

Splitting up the data solves a problem. When you download a video or other large file, there has to be gigabytes of data sent to you.

This is a problem because if the data was all sent as one large stream, it would block other users: the routers would be busy trying to send your file, so they couldn't look at theirs.

How this problem was solved

This problem was solved by, as you have already seen, splitting it apart. Each tiny part, a few bytes, contains two parts.

The first part is the header. This contains information like who it is from, and where it is going.

The next part is the payload. This is the actual data that is trying to be sent from device to device.

The header contains who it is in a similar way that postage has return addresses: if it gets stuck or sent back, it can return to the sender.

What is a router?

A router is a device that forwards packets between networks, including internetworks like the Internet (Wikipedia).

Why can't you just send your data straight to the internet?

This is like trying to send and recieve mail without a mailbox and being banned from the mail office: you can't really send others anything, and there is no way for people to send you their mail.

Another reason is like how there is mail offices in the first place. Many homes [devices] send their mail [packets] to the mail office [router] to process in bulk - your device isn't engineered for packet sending, but routers are.

Using the Internet

Browsers

Browsers are the main form of internet usage for the average person. They let you send and recieve data through webpages, as well as view and interact with them.

An example of a webpage you can interact with and view is this page itself. Your browser takes the HTML and the CSS of the webpage, as well as some JavaScript, and then displays those three together.
